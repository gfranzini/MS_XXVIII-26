Methods

As mentioned in the About page of this website, this digital edition is the result of a study of the numerous digital edition projects contained in the Catalogue of Digital Editions but also of personal conversations with creators of digital editions. The simplicity of the digital edition of MS XXVIII(26) was designed to support access to, and reuse of, its underlying data. By avoiding the adoption of advanced technologies or languages, it hopes to minimise the risk of obsolescence. There are, of course, drawbacks to using a simple HTML framework, namely searching and streamline updating, but it fulfills the purpose of this project.

Editorial

I encode the manuscript in accordance with EpiDoc standards, a subset of the TEI framework. Only a handful of EpiDoc tags are used to transcribe the text (LIST). The aim is not to encode every single detail but to provide a minimum reading aid.

I'm not producing full transcriptions of the witnesses referenced but I provide the standard readings published in the Corpus Scriptorum Ecclesiasticorum Latinorum (CSEL) edition of De civitate Dei (volume 40). The PDF version of CSEL 40 published by the Internet Archive is split into part 1 and part 2. A EpiDoc XML version of CSEL 40 is available here.

I strive to provide both XML and TXT versions of the transcription for different users and uses. This edition targets researchers, students and the interested public.

Technical

No search function because there's no underlying database. It's all pure HTML Bootstrap-CSS with short snippets of JQuery (image zooming). Reasons:

1. Lighter project, easier maintenance as I do not depend on experts. 2. HTML/CSS less subject to versioning conflicts and deprecation. 3. The current alternative to string searching is to download the .txt versions of the transcriptions and search those.

Current issues

Bugs and issues with the edition are recorded in this project's GitHub repository. One current bug is the HTML display of macrons, which is not always consistent.

Value

To the best of my knowledge, this is the only project that provides a (digital) transcription of MS XXVIII(26) and the first attempt to make its contents openly available for download and reuse. Secondly, this edition seeks to dynamically link MS XXVIII(26) to other relevant digital initiatives, breaking the constraints of existing print publications and bringing readers closer to the primary sources.
